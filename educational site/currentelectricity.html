Current Electricity


Electric Charge and Current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge in a conductor...

Formula: q = I × t
  • q = charge in coulombs (C)
  • I = current in amperes (A)
  • t = time in seconds (s)

Example: If 3 A of current flows for 4 seconds, q = 3 × 4 = 12 C.

Electrical Resistance

Resistance is the opposition that a material offers...

Formula: R = V / I

Factors affecting resistance:

  • Material of the conductor
  • Length (longer = more resistance)
  • Cross-sectional area (wider = less resistance)
  • Temperature (higher = more resistance in metals)

Potential Difference

The potential difference between two points...

Formula: V = W / q

Unit: Volt (V)

Electromotive Force (EMF)

EMF is the maximum potential difference provided...

Formula: E = W / q

Note: EMF includes internal resistance of the source.

Ohm's Law

Ohm’s Law states that the current flowing through...

Formula: V = IR

Example: If R = 5 Ω and I = 2 A, then V = 2 × 5 = 10 V.

Power in Electric Circuits

Electrical power is the rate at which energy is transferred...

Formulas:
  • P = VI
  • P = I²R
  • P = V²/R

Unit: Watt (W)

Energy in Electric Circuits

The electrical energy used or supplied over a period of time is:

Formulas:
  • E = Pt
  • Alternatively: E = VIt

Unit: Joules (J)

Example: If V = 10 V, I = 2 A, and t = 3 s, then E = 10 × 2 × 3 = 60 J.

Series and Parallel Circuits

Series:

  • Same current flows through all components.
  • Total resistance: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …
  • Voltage is divided across components.

Parallel:

  • Same voltage across each component.
  • Current is divided among branches.
  • Total resistance: 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + …

Internal Resistance and Terminal Voltage

Batteries and power sources have internal resistance (r)...

Formula: V = E − Ir
  • V = terminal voltage
  • E = EMF
  • I = current
  • r = internal resistance
